How to Bake Sourdough Bread Using Wild Yeast

By: Penelope

Introduction

Sourdough bread is a staple in many households, and for good reason. It's delicious, nutritious, and has a long shelf life. If you're looking to learn how to make this bread using wild yeast, you're in the right place. Baking with wild yeast can seem daunting, but it's actually quite simple. In this guide, we'll walk you through the steps of sourdough bread baking using wild yeast, so you can make your own delicious loaves at home. First, let's talk about what wild yeast is. Wild yeast is a type of yeast that is naturally present in the air around us. It's the same yeast that's used to make sourdough bread, but it's not as reliable as commercial yeast. That's because wild yeast can vary in its strength and activity level, depending on the time of year and the location. This can make sourdough bread baking a bit tricky, but it also makes it more exciting and rewarding. To get started, you'll need a sourdough starter. A sourdough starter is a mixture of flour and water that has been left to ferment for several days. The fermentation process allows the wild yeast to grow and populate the mixture, creating a natural leavening agent. Once you have your starter, you'll need to feed it regularly to keep it alive and active. Next, you'll need to mix your dough. Sourdough bread dough is made with flour, water, and salt. You'll need to mix these ingredients together and then let the dough rise. This can take several hours, depending on the strength of your wild yeast and the temperature of your kitchen. Finally, you'll need to bake your bread. Sourdough bread is typically baked in a hot, steamy oven to create a crispy crust and a soft, chewy interior. You can use a Dutch oven or a baking stone to achieve the desired results. Overall, baking sourdough bread using wild yeast may seem intimidating at first, but with a little patience and practice, anyone can do it. Follow these steps, and soon you'll be enjoying delicious and nutritious loaves of sourdough bread straight out of your own oven.

1. Create a Sourdough Starter

Are you tired of bland, store-bought bread? Want to try your hand at baking your bread but don't want to use commercial yeast? Look no further than sourdough bread made with wild yeast. Sourdough bread has a deep, complex flavor and a chewy texture that commercial yeast bread can't match. And the best part? You can make your sourdough starter at home using just a few ingredients. To create a sourdough starter, all you need is flour and water. The key is to use whole wheat or rye flour, as these types of flour contain more natural yeast and bacteria than all-purpose flour. Mix equal parts of flour and water in a jar or bowl until you have a thick batter. Cover the container with a breathable lid, like a kitchen towel, and leave it on your countertop for 24 hours. After 24 hours, you should start to see small bubbles forming on the surface of your starter. This is a sign that the natural yeast and bacteria in the flour are beginning to ferment. Stir the mixture and discard about half of it, then add equal parts flour and water to the remaining mixture. Cover it with the lid again, and leave it for another 24 hours. Repeat this process every day for about a week, discarding half of the starter and adding fresh flour and water. Eventually, you'll notice that your starter is alive and active, with a bubbly texture and a sour smell. This means that the natural yeast and bacteria in your starter are strong enough to use in bread baking. Now that you've got your sourdough starter, it's time to start making bread. Keep your starter in the fridge and feed it once a week with equal parts flour and water. When you're ready to bake bread, simply mix the starter with flour, water, and salt to make a dough. Let the dough rise overnight, then bake it in a preheated oven for 30-40 minutes. With a little patience and practice, you can make delicious, homemade sourdough bread using wild yeast. Start by creating your sourdough starter, and before you know it, you'll be a sourdough expert.

2. Mixing the Dough

Mixing the Dough: The Key to Perfecting Your Sourdough Bread If you're a bread lover, then nothing is quite like the aroma and taste of a freshly baked sourdough loaf. But making that perfect sourdough bread can sometimes seem like an elusive feat. Fear not! With a few basic tips, you can become a master baker in no time. One crucial step in making sourdough bread is the mixing of the dough. Getting the dough mixture right is key to achieving that perfectly textured, chewy, and tangy loaf. Firstly, it's essential to understand the different stages of dough mixing. There are a few stages of mixing that occur when making sourdough bread. These include hydration, autolyse, bulk fermentation, and shaping. Get these stages right, and you'll be on your way to baking the best-loved bread you've ever tasted. Secondly, it’s important to mix the dough enough but not too much. You don't want to overmix or undermix your dough, as both can have a significant impact on the final product. Additionally, it’s important to recognize that sourdough bread is unique compared to other bread varieties because it requires a longer fermentation process. This is because sourdough is leavened using wild yeast, which can result in a slower rising time. It's important to be patient and allow enough time for the dough to develop fully. Making sourdough bread with wild yeast is a complex process that takes time, patience, and practice. But mastering the dough mixing stage of the process is the first step. With a little knowledge and experience, you'll be making the perfect loaf of sourdough bread in no time. So, let's get mixing and start baking that perfect sourdough bread!

3. Shaping the Dough

If you've successfully followed the first two steps of feeding and fermenting your sourdough starter, it's time for the exciting part - shaping the dough! Shaping the dough plays a crucial role in creating the perfect loaf of sourdough bread. It's all about developing tension on the surface of the dough to ensure that it rises uniformly during the baking process. Before shaping the dough, you need to ensure it's properly fermented. A well-fermented dough is easier to shape and handle. You'll know your dough is ready when it has doubled in size, has an airy texture, and has a slightly sour smell. To shape the dough, first, gently deflate it and transfer it onto a lightly floured surface. Fold one-third of the dough towards the center and then repeat with the other side. Next, take the top part of the dough and fold it towards the center, then the bottom part towards the center as well. Pinch the seams together and flip the dough over, so the seams are on the bottom. Now you need to create tension on the surface of the dough. Place your hands at the bottom edge of the dough and gently pull it towards you, then tuck it under the dough. Repeat this process a few times until you feel a tightness on the surface of the dough. Shape the dough into a boule or batard shape, depending on your preference. Once you've shaped the dough, transfer it to a lined proofing basket or a kitchen towel-lined bowl. Let it rest for another two to three hours, depending on the temperature and humidity of your kitchen. You'll know your dough is ready when it holds its shape and has a slightly domed appearance. Shaping the dough is an essential step in mastering the art of sourdough bread baking. With practice, you'll develop your technique and create beautiful, crusty loaves that are sure to impress. Happy baking!

4. Baking the Bread

The moment has arrived! After hours of meticulous preparation and fermentation, your sourdough bread is ready to be baked. Aromas of freshly baked bread will soon fill your home, and the satisfaction of making your own bread will leave you feeling like a true artisan. But how do you turn your sticky dough into a beautifully crusty and soft loaf of bread? Fear not, these tips will guide you through the baking process and ensure your bread is a success every time. First, preheat your oven to 450°F (232°C) and place a Dutch oven or heavy-lidded pot inside for 30 minutes to get it scorching hot. While the pot heats up, prepare your dough by removing it from the proofing basket and placing it on a flour-dusted piece of parchment paper. With a sharp knife or blade, score the dough's surface to allow it to rise during the baking process. Next, carefully remove the pot from the hot oven and place the dough, along with the parchment paper, inside. Cover the pot with the lid and bake for 20 minutes. After 20 minutes, remove the lid and continue baking for another 25 to 30 minutes until the bread is golden brown and the crust is crispy. To check if your bread is ready, tap the bottom of the loaf; it should sound hollow. Once you've removed the bread from the pot, allow it to cool for at least an hour before slicing. But wait, your bread-baking journey doesn't have to end here. Try adding different seasonings to your dough, experimenting with different flours, or shaping your loaf differently to achieve a unique result every time. So, dust off your flour-covered apron and continue to explore the exciting world of sourdough bread-baking!

5. Cooling and Storing

The final step in the art of baking sourdough bread is cooling and storing. Once removed from the oven, your bread will continue to cook for a few more minutes. As soon as it's out of the oven, use oven mitts to take the bread out of the baking container, and set it on a wire rack to allow it to cool. The cooling process is crucial as it finishes the baking process, sets the crust, and allows the bread to become firm enough to slice. If you slice the bread too early, the crumb or the interior of the bread will be damp and gummy, while the crust will be soft, and this will ruin your experience. Leave the bread to cool for a minimum of one hour or more, depending on the size of the loaf. If you're unsure, tap the bottom of the bread, and if it sounds hollow, then it's ready. When storing sourdough bread, you should know that it does not contain any preservatives, so it will not have the same shelf life as store-bought bread. Store in a paper bag to allow the bread to breathe, or wrap it loosely in plastic wrap or foil, leaving it unwrapped at the top so that the crust does not become soft. Avoid storing the bread in the refrigerator, as this will cause the bread to become stale faster than if kept at room temperature. You can freeze sourdough bread, preferably sliced, then wrapped in a double layer of plastic or foil. When ready to eat, reheat in the oven or toaster, and it will taste as fresh as the day you baked it. By following these tips, you can fully enjoy the flavour and texture of your homemade sourdough bread for days to come as you share it with your family and friends. Happy Baking!

6. Troubleshooting Common Issues

Sourdough bread baking is an art that has been perfected over generations. Baking with wild yeast can be tricky, but once mastered, you can create flavorful and nutritious bread with a crispy crust and soft core. However, even seasoned bakers can run into issues that can negatively impact the final product. In this article, we will address the most common issues that arise when baking sourdough bread with wild yeast. From over-proofing to under-proofing, we'll provide clear explanations and practical tips on how to avoid mistakes and guarantee a successful bake every time. One common issue when baking sourdough bread is the formation of a dense and heavy crumb. The reason for this is often over-proofing. Over-proofing usually happens when the dough is left to rise for too long, causing the yeast to consume all available sugars in the dough. To avoid over-proofing, pay attention to the dough's rise and poke test to determine when it is ready. Another common issue is a lack of oven spring or the dough's inability to rise in the oven. This can be due to several factors, including under-proofing, poor shaping, or inadequate steam in the oven. To ensure proper oven spring, follow the recommended proofing times, shape the dough correctly, and use a baking vessel to create steam in the oven. Other issues include a burnt crust, an uneven bake, and a sour taste. We will discuss tips to address these issues and more, giving you the confidence to tackle any challenges that arise when baking sourdough bread. Join us on this journey of perfecting the art of baking sourdough bread. Whether you're a beginner or an experienced baker, learning to troubleshoot common issues will help you achieve a perfect loaf every time.
